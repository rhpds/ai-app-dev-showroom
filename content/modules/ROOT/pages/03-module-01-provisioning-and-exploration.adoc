= Module 1: Provisioning and exploration
:source-highlighter: rouge
:toc: macro
:toclevels: 1

It's your first day working on AI initiatives at Parasol Insurance. Normally, getting a new developer productive takes weeks -- setting up environments, requesting access, installing tools, and navigating endless documentation. But Parasol has adopted Red Hat Developer Hub to eliminate that friction.

In this module, you'll self-service a fully configured development workstation, explore the Red Hat build of Podman Desktop and its extensions, and get your first AI chatbot up and running locally -- all in about 40 minutes.

== Learning objectives

By the end of this module, you'll be able to:

* Use Red Hat Developer Hub to self-service a RHEL 10 virtual machine with pre-installed developer tools
* Navigate the Red Hat build of Podman Desktop and explore its extensions
* Install and use the AI Lab extension to work with AI models locally
* Instantiate an AI chatbot recipe and interact with a local model

== Exercise 1: Provision your development environment with Red Hat Developer Hub

Your team lead has told you: "Just go to Developer Hub, pick the AI developer workstation template, and you'll have everything you need." No tickets, no waiting, no manual setup.

You'll use Red Hat Developer Hub to provision a RHEL 10 virtual machine that comes pre-configured with the Red Hat build of Podman Desktop and other essential tools.

. Open Red Hat Developer Hub in your browser:

+
NOTE: If you don't see this link in your environment, you can access Red Hat Developer Hub from the OpenShift console at link:{openshift_console_url}[{openshift_console_url}^] by navigating to the Developer Hub link in the application menu.

. Log in using your credentials:
+
* **Username**: `{user}`
* **Password**: `{password}`

// TODO: Screenshot of template catalog with AI Developer Workstation highlighted

== Exercise 2: Explore the Red Hat build of Podman Desktop

Now that your workstation is provisioned, let's explore the Red Hat build of Podman Desktop -- the primary tool Parasol developers use for container-native development on the desktop.

=== Connect to your virtual machine

. Open a VNC session to your RHEL 10 virtual machine. You can access it from the link provided in the provisioning success page, or through the OpenShift console:
+
link:{openshift_console_url}/k8s/ns/{user}-devspaces/kubevirt.io~v1~VirtualMachine[OpenShift Console - Virtual Machines^]
+
// TODO: Screenshot of VNC console connection

. Log in to the virtual machine desktop using the provided credentials.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of RHEL 10 desktop after login
image::rhel-desktop.png[RHEL 10 desktop environment,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

=== Launch Podman Desktop

. Find and launch the **Red Hat build of Podman Desktop** from the application menu or desktop shortcut.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of launching Podman Desktop
image::podman-desktop-launch.png[Launching Podman Desktop from RHEL 10,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Once Podman Desktop opens, take a moment to explore the interface:

=== Explore extensions

. Click on **Extensions** in the left navigation to see what's available. The Red Hat build of Podman Desktop supports a rich extension ecosystem.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of extensions page
image::podman-desktop-extensions.png[Podman Desktop extensions page,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Browse through the available extensions. You'll notice extensions for:
+
* Container registries
* Kubernetes and OpenShift integration
* Podman machine management
* And more
+
TIP: Extensions are how Podman Desktop stays lightweight while still supporting specialized workflows like AI development.

== Exercise 3: Install the AI Lab extension and run a chatbot recipe

At Parasol, developers need a safe, local way to experiment with AI -- no more shadow AI using random third-party services. The AI Lab extension for Podman Desktop provides exactly this: a governed, local-first approach to AI experimentation.

=== Install the AI Lab extension

. In the Extensions view, search for **AI Lab** or locate it in the catalog.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of AI Lab extension in the catalog
image::ai-lab-extension-catalog.png[AI Lab extension in the catalog,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Click **Install** to add the AI Lab extension to your Podman Desktop.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of AI Lab installation progress
image::ai-lab-installing.png[Installing AI Lab extension,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Once installed, you'll see a new **AI Lab** icon in the left navigation.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of AI Lab icon appearing in navigation
image::ai-lab-nav.png[AI Lab icon in Podman Desktop navigation,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

=== Explore the AI Lab

. Click on the **AI Lab** icon to open it. Take a moment to explore:
+
* **Catalog**: Pre-built AI recipes and models
* **Models**: Available AI models you can run locally
* **Playground**: A place to experiment with models interactively
+
// TODO: Screenshot of AI Lab main page
image::ai-lab-main.png[AI Lab main page,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

=== Try the AI playground

. Navigate to the **Playground** section in AI Lab.

. Select a small model from the available list. Since you're running locally, choose one of the smaller models to keep things responsive.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of model selection in playground
image::ai-lab-playground-model.png[Selecting a model in the AI playground,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Once the model loads, try chatting with it. Type a simple question like:
+
[source,text]
----
What is Parasol Insurance and what services do they provide?
----
+
// TODO: Screenshot of a chat exchange in the playground
image::ai-lab-playground-chat.png[Chatting with a model in the AI playground,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Notice the response quality and speed. The local model works, but it's quite small -- the responses may be generic or slow. That's expected. You'll address this limitation in Module 2.
+
TIP: The AI playground is useful for quick experimentation. For building actual applications, you'll use the recipe catalog.

=== Instantiate the chatbot recipe

. Navigate to the **Catalog** section in AI Lab.

. Find the **Chatbot** recipe in the catalog. This is a pre-built application template that provides a web-based chat interface backed by an AI model.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of the chatbot recipe in the catalog
image::ai-lab-catalog-chatbot.png[Chatbot recipe in the AI Lab catalog,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Click on the chatbot recipe to see its details, then click **Start** to instantiate it.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of the chatbot recipe detail page with Start button
image::ai-lab-chatbot-start.png[Starting the chatbot recipe,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Wait for the recipe to pull the required model and start the application. This may take a few minutes as it downloads the model and builds the containers.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of the recipe starting/pulling
image::ai-lab-chatbot-pulling.png[Chatbot recipe pulling model and starting,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Once the recipe is running, click the link to open the chatbot application in your browser.
+
// TODO: Screenshot of the running chatbot application
image::ai-lab-chatbot-running.png[Chatbot application running locally,link=self,window=blank,width=700]

. Try asking the chatbot a few questions:
+
[source,text]
----
Hello! Can you help me with an insurance claim?
----
+
[source,text]
----
What types of coverage does Parasol Insurance offer?
----

. Notice that the chatbot responds, but the answers are generic -- it doesn't know anything specific about Parasol Insurance, and the local model is quite small with limited performance. In the next module, you'll customize this application and connect it to a more powerful model.

=== Verify

Confirm that:

* The AI Lab extension is installed in Podman Desktop
* The chatbot recipe is running locally
* You can interact with the chatbot through the web interface

You've successfully completed the provisioning and exploration module -- and it only took a fraction of the time Parasol's old onboarding process would have required.

**What you accomplished:**

* Self-serviced a fully configured RHEL 10 development workstation through Red Hat Developer Hub -- no tickets, no waiting
* Explored the Red Hat build of Podman Desktop and its extension ecosystem
* Installed the AI Lab extension and experimented with a local AI model

**Key takeaways:**

* Red Hat Developer Hub eliminates the developer onboarding bottleneck with self-service templates
* Podman Desktop provides a powerful, extensible desktop container environment
* The AI Lab extension gives developers a governed, local-first approach to AI experimentation -- eliminating the need for shadow AI services
* Local models are useful for experimentation but have performance limitations
